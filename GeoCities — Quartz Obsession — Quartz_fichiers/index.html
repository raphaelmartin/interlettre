GeoCities

January 20, 2018

In the beginning

Facebook: Where to begin with what it hath wrought?

Last week, the social media giant (which has almost as many users as Christianity has followers) announced that it’s going old school, deprioritizing publisher- and brand-promoted posts in favor of more “quality interactions” between human beings.

For many critics, the correction is both overdue and underwhelming. “The short-term, dopamine-driven feedback loops we’ve created are destroying how society works,” Chamath Palihapitiya, formerly Facebook’s VP of user growth, said last fall. He’s just one of the Silicon Valley insiders who have spoken out about the ways tech companies exploit people’s psychological vulnerabilities.

Facebook has also been snapping up companies like tbh, which lets people send each other anonymous positive feedback. The fighting spirit of tbh can be traced to a different era: What if GeoCities, a famously positive web hosting service, had been the platform that took off instead? The web, and the world, might be a very different place indeed.

Origin story

The people in your neighborhood

Like a proto-social network, GeoCities was a hosting service that let users build their own web pages but also organize into neighborhoods dedicated to shared interests and identities. If you were into aliens, you’d probably build your virtual home in “Area51.” If you were an art kid, you’d go to “SoHo.” There were also neighborhoods for extreme sports (“Pipeline”) and the LGBT community (“WestHollywood”)—one of the first inclusive spaces online.

Even if you weren’t a member, you’d probably recognize that infamous aesthetic: “Brimming with blinking, moving, garishly colored text and animated dancing cats, GeoCities made MySpace look like a bastion of elegance and restraint,” is how Farhad Manjoo put it.

But at the time, all that blinking signified something to users: maximum creative freedom, the ability to build an online presence that expressed who you really were, and the opportunity to connect with others like you.

Timeline

1993: GeoCities formally launches. Co-founders David Bohnett and John Rezner’s idea was originally called Beverly Hills Internet, inspired by the Trojan Room coffee pot. “I had the idea that we would create a series of communities around themed webcams. I thought if we give away free web pages to people to create content the webcams would initially draw the traffic, then people would create web pages around these themes,” Bohnett told Quartz.

1997: GeoCities surpasses 1 million users, equivalent to at least 7% of internet users at the time.

1998: GeoCities goes public, listing on Nasdaq at $17 a share before nearly doubling that price in less than 24 hours. The IPO valued the company at $521 million, or about $750 million in present-day money.

1999: A still unprofitable GeoCities is acquired for $4.7 billion by Yahoo, which has a knack for poorly-timed purchases (sorry, Tumblr, you’re not special). The Wall Street Journal described it as one of Yahoo’s signature “deals from hell.” “Having a page on there became a synonym for dilettantism and bad taste,” Olia Lialina and Dragan Espenschied summarize on One Terabyte of Kilobyte Age. “The time of personal home pages was counted, being replaced with profiles on social networks.”

2009: GeoCities is shuttered, as Yahoo pulls the plug on the core North American business and redirects curious users to its Small Business service. GeoCities lives on in Japan, however. As of last year, at least 17 neighborhoods remain, hosting everything from AI singers to Japanese folklore.

Department of jargon

“Geo Cities” or “Gee-OS-ities?”

Believe it or not, a debate still rages about the official pronunciation. EHow’s video on creating your own website uses the latter. Co-founder David Bohnett goes with the former, mostly because the idea for GeoCities sprang from a region-focused approach to fostering online communities.

Fun fact

The classic “under construction” digging man gif —among many other animated icons—was created by an early adopter named Steve Kangas, who quit his job in 1995 to spend 10 hours a day online, surfing and creating web projects.

deep roots

Credit where credit is due

At a time when most online interactions were built around rapid-fire surface-level relationships that unfolded in chat rooms, GeoCities stood out as a new model for how to experience digital spaces. “In GeoCities, I am given my own homepage, where the friends I met on GeoCities come over… This experience is similar to friends coming over to your apartment, hanging out and having a cup of java,” writes Annie Chen in a University of Michigan paper about the platform.

At Slate, Farhad Manjoo writes that GeoCities deserves more credit than it gets for this reimagination of what the web could provide to users. “Today, few of us think of the Web as a simple source for information; it’s also a place for dissemination, the place where we share life’s most intimate details. In other words, it’s for ‘habitation’—and GeoCities helped start that trend.”

It also gave people without any expertise a place to both play and learn. Adam Ostrow, a digital media exec, built his first GeoCities page in celebration of Atlanta Braves legend Chipper Jones. “Building websites, creating digital content and understanding how to engage online communities… Having these experiences as a kid, on GeoCities, made those skills second nature to me,” Ostrow tells Quartz.

In the cul-de-sac

One Terabyte of Kilobyte Age, a database of preserved screenshots from GeoCities sites created by artists Olia Lialina and Dragan Espenschied, contains a massive data dump of GeoCities home pages lovingly preserved by the Archive Team.

Some of its compilations, like this collection of screenshots that catch the first days and weeks after 9/11, are profound portraits of the moment. Others archive the granular customization options GeoCities offered. Clicking through to a GeoCities page could transform your cursor from an arrow or a gloved hand to a magic wand; a RealPlayer plug-in piped in MIDI versions of the site owner’s favorite tunes. Pages could be built like digital houses: Click on a door, and you’d be led into a hallway with hyperlinked stairs and rooms.

It was all a very stark contrast to today’s sleek plug-and-play web hosting platforms and the tepid blue of Facebook’s feed. “Users have found new ways to work with the web of today, especially social media, but there is almost no sense of ownership anymore,” Lialina told Quartz.

The way we 💻 now

Since GeoCities swept the ‘net, web hosting services have only grown more sophisticated. GoDaddy’s risqué ads have snared it countless users and Squarespace is probably the reason your favorite podcast is still around. But those longing for that sweet Web 1.0 aesthetic need only dust off their Earthlink trial disks before heading to NeoCities or GeoCities.ws—two prominent platforms that explicitly evoke the GeoCities sites of yore.
