**New Group Scrutinizes AI Chip Claims (The Information)**  
Chips that speed up artificial intelligence software have recently become all the rage in the semiconductor industry. But it’s hard for companies buying those chips to determine how well the hardware performs and how they compare against each other. Lots of claims around chip performance are thrown around without much substance behind them.
A new group— consisting of both industry and academia—has come together to tackle this issue. Called MLPerf, the new benchmark measures the speed of the latest software and hardware to train deep learning networks to perform tasks like recognizing objects and translating languages.  
The tool was designed in partnership with tech giants Google, Intel, AMD and Baidu as well as academic institutions Stanford and Harvard. Two AI chip startups—SambaNova and Wave Computing—also took part.
One glaring missing member in this founding group is Nvidia, the graphics chip maker who currently has almost complete control over the booming market for training deep learning networks. The companies involves in this group are eager to capture some of that growth from Nvidia, and this new tool could help them. —Aaron

**A virtual scavenger hunt could lead to the first useful home robots**  
Researchers from Facebook and Georgia Tech have created a digital model of a house to train AI.
The contest: A virtual agent is tasked with looking for something in a simulated home after parsing a natural-language question. Questions would be something like “What color is the car?” or “Where is the coffee table?”
Solving the puzzle: Finding the answer requires an agent to understand the question and then explore the virtual space in search of the relevant object. Over time, the program figures out the best areas of the house to explore to get the answer.
Why it matters: The research may feed into more futuristic projects. Future home robots might use AI software that has learned facts like what a bedroom is, or where one might be found by exploring virtual homes first.


**The US is approaching a defining moment for cryptocurrency innovation**  
Federal regulators are grappling with a simple question: should the world’s second-most-valuable cryptocurrency, be regulated like a stock?
Behind the scenes: The Wall Street Journal reports that regulators are examining whether ether, the currency native to the Ethereum blockchain, should be subject to the same strict regulations as stocks and bonds.
The central question: It all hinges on whether Ethereum’s developers are in a position to influence its currency’s value. People in the “no” camp argue that the decentralized computer network the currency is based on prevents that. At MIT Technology Review’s Business of Blockchain event last week, Gary Gensler, former head of the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, argued in the affirmative.
Is crypto winter coming? Also last week, the New York Times reported that a group of venture capitalists has lobbied the US Securities and Exchange Commission to create a “safe harbor” for some currencies. Whichever way it goes, billions of dollars are at stake.

**Chinese tech firms are throwing out applicants over the age of 30**  
Ageism at tech companies is forcing people who elsewhere would be entering the prime of their careers out of the industry.
The “30+ middle age crisis”: Three-quarters of tech workers in China are younger than 30, and recruiters are reinforcing this: some are instructed to cut off applicants at age 35.
Why? As one recruiter told Bloomberg, the perception is that “most people in their 30s are married and have to take care of their family—they’re not able to focus on the high-intensity work.” Younger workers also cost companies less.
It's not just China’s problem: In March, IBM faced allegations of persistent age discrimination in the US. The biggest difference is that in China, discriminating based on age is legal.
The paradox: As Bloomberg points out, most of China’s landmark tech companies were started by people over 30. But, possibly due to ageist policies, that’s changing: more and more rising tech companies are led by founders in their 20s.

**Un projet de budget européen contesté  
Que prévoit le projet de budget européen présenté hier ?**  
La Commission européenne a présenté hier un projet de budget de 1 279 milliards d’euros pour la période 2021-2027, en hausse de 17 % par rapport à la période actuelle. Le retrait du Royaume-Uni de l’Union européenne (UE), qui prendra effet en 2019, sera compensé par une hausse de la contribution des 27 autres États membres. Les plus gros postes de dépenses resteront la politique agricole commune (PAC) et la politique de cohésion, destinée au développement des régions les plus pauvres de l’UE, mais le projet prévoit des baisses de 5 % et 6 % pour ces deux programmes. Le texte propose une augmentation de 50 % du budget de la recherche et un doublement de celui du programme Erasmus. De nouvelles lignes budgétaires qui n’existaient pas sont également créées pour le soutien de la zone euro, la politique européenne de défense et la gestion des migrations.
Pourquoi le gouvernement français critique-t-il ce projet de budget ?  
Le ministère de l’Agriculture a critiqué hier dans un communiqué la baisse de 5 % du budget de l’agriculture dans l’UE proposée par la Commission. Ces fonds sont en majeure partie versés sous la forme d’aides directes aux agriculteurs, principalement calculées en fonction de la taille des exploitations. « Une telle baisse, drastique, massive et aveugle, est simplement inenvisageable », dénonce le ministère de l’Agriculture, pointant « un risque sans précédent sur la viabilité des exploitations ». La France est de loin le pays qui perçoit le plus d’aides au titre de la PAC (près de 9 milliards d’euros par an), devant l’Allemagne (6,4 milliards) et l’Espagne (6 milliards).  
Quelles sont les étapes avant l’adoption du budget ?
Du 29 mai au 12 juin, la Commission européenne publiera le détail de ses propositions dans chaque domaine. Ce projet de budget pluriannuel détermine les montants maximaux que l’UE peut dépenser chaque année. Des négociations s’engageront alors entre la Commission européenne, les États membres et le Parlement européen. Un accord du Parlement européen et des 27 États membres de l’UE à l’unanimité doit être trouvé pour adopter ce budget pluriannuel. Jean-Claude Juncker espère y parvenir avant les prochaines élections européennes, prévues en mai 2019. Un changement de majorité au Parlement à cette occasion pourrait remettre en cause les négociations si aucun accord n’a été entériné à cette date.

**Amazon Plans To Measure People With Body Scanners To Improve Clothing Sizing (The Wall Street Journal)**  
Amazon is inviting people to one of its New York offices to scan their bodies 10 times over the course of 20 weeks in an effort to better understand how to size clothing, according to the Wall Street Journal. The idea is that if the company can better predict how clothes will fit people, it could reduce the likelihood of returns.
Amazon has launched several private label apparel brands and is attempting to increase its presence in fashion. The company’s “Prime Wardrobe” service, for example, lets people fill up boxes with clothes, try them on at home, pay for what they want and return whatever they don’t want to keep.   
The body-scanning move shows how high the e-commerce giant’s ambitions are in the apparel space. If Amazon knows your style based on past purchases and can identify what clothes will fit you best, it can perhaps recommend pants or dresses to you. 
Wall Street analysts have long cited the Amazon threat to Stitch Fix, which picks out clothes for its customers using algorithms and human stylists. This is the kind of news they were waiting for. —Priya

**Airbnb Keeps Getting Pinned for Rising Rents in New York (The New York Times)**  
In a new report, the top fiscal officer in New York City concluded that Airbnb rentals contributed to about one-fifth of the increase in rents in hot Brooklyn and Manhattan neighborhoods between 2009 and 2016. **TBS Shortens Conan O'Brien’s Talkshow to Half an Hour (Hollywood Reporter)**  
A decade ago this might be considered something of a demotion. Conan O'Brien, the longest tenured talkshow host on TV, is getting his show cut in half to 30 minutes. The explanation here is that so much of the show’s viewing comes online that it makes more sense to build a show that relies on bitesized segments rather than keep the traditional format.
And therein lies the reason that a shorter show may not be the punishment it might have looked like. Television has changed over the past five years and, for talkshows in particular, much of the viewing takes place on social media. Embracing this shift is an interesting turn for O'Brien, who once cared so deeply about the traditions of late night television that he refused NBC’s plan to move the Tonight Show to midnight during his short term hosting the program. Now he’s the vanguard of the migration of TV talkshows into digital properties.  
Of course, the other rationale here is that digital is a cheaper and less profitable outlet and his show was too expensive for TBS given its meager viewership.  Conan, despite his digital success, is a less valuable property now for Turner than when he signed in 2010. But given the pace of cord cutting, so is Turner. So something had to change. —Tom

**Question for Snap: Why the Rush to IPO? (Los Angeles Times)**  
Snap stock fell another 3% on Thursday to yet a new low, making this column in the LA Times timely. It poses the question I also found puzzling: why was Snap so keen to go public, in an era when other tech firms were putting off IPOs? Those other firms may sometimes be waiting too long. But in Snap’s case, given the enormous losses it was (and still is) sustaining and its relative youth, its IPO seemed obviously too soon. Evan Spiegel didn’t seem to grasp that going public meant intense scrutiny, which can be devastating for a company whose growth is falling short of expectations. That can create a negative feedback loop, with critical coverage in the media affecting both employee morale and attitudes of customers like advertisers. All that is now coming true for Snap.   
Spiegel can be forgiven this error of judgement given his youth—he’s 27 after all. The problem is he has control of the company. Investors have to take that into account. Why would anyone buy shares in Snap when it is controlled by such an inexperienced person? If Spiegel was smart, he’d bring in a veteran executive and step aside. 
—Martin

**Is Xiaomi Really an Internet Company? (The Information)**  
Chinese smartphone maker Xiaomi loves to describe itself as an internet company. But according to Xiaomi’s IPO prospectus filed on Tuesday, more than 90% of its 2017 revenue came from hardware, specifically the sale of smartphones as well as other gadgets and appliances. Internet services, which include advertising, accounted for 8.6%—down from 9.6% in 2016.  
Xiaomi executives have always said that the company’s business model differs drastically from that of a traditional hardware company. After selling its gadgets at low prices with thin margins, Xiaomi can make money by selling online services to the users of its products, they have said. Xiaomi’s IPO is expected to raise at least $10 billion at a valuation as high as $100 billion, according to Bloomberg. To justify an internet company-like valuation, Xiaomi needs to show more evidence that its internet services are thriving and the company really is much more than a hardware maker.   —Juro
