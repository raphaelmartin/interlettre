Institutions

FR Jean-Louis Borloo propose de créer un corps de hauts fonctionnaires des quartiers – Dans son rapport sur « la situation des quartiers prioritaires de la politique de la ville », remis au Premier ministre jeudi 26 avril, l’ancien ministre de l’Écologie lie notamment les problèmes dans ces quartiers au profil des hauts fonctionnaires. Il constate « une trop grande homogénéité » des diplômés de l’ENA et le « caractère formaté de la formation de cette élite ». Jean-Louis Borloo suggère donc de mettre en place une grande école pour former de futurs hauts fonctionnaires venus des quartiers. Ces derniers auraient le statut de fonctionnaire stagiaire de l’ENA. Ils seraient rémunérés pendant leur scolarité à hauteur de 1 700 euros brut par mois pendant les 3 années de leur scolarité. L’organisation pratique serait entre les mains des instituts régionaux d’administration. Le président de la République se prononcera en mai sur le sujet. Le texte intégral du rapport.

« Fake news »

UE Bruxelles donne rendez-vous aux plateformes à l’automne – La Commission va mettre en place un forum sur la lutte contre la désinformation qui réunira le secteur de la publicité, la société civile, les médias et les plateformes, explique-t-elle dans sa communication, présentée le 26 avril. Ce groupe rédigera, d’ici à juillet, un code de bonnes pratiques (transparence, fermeture de faux comptes…), dont les résultats devront être visibles dès octobre 2018. D’ici décembre, la Commission évaluera « les progrès ». Si elle les juge insatisfaisants, elle prendra des mesures supplémentaires, « y compris réglementaires ». La communication met également l’accent sur l’éducation au média et annonce la création d’un réseau européen de fact-checkers. Le dossier de presse.

UE Les représentants de la société civile vont rédiger leur propre rapport sur les « fake news » – Dans un communiqué commun, les ONG de défense des droits Edri, Access Now et Liberties estiment que la stratégie de la Commission représente un risque pour les droits de l’homme, notamment la liberté d’expression et la protection de la vie privée. Pour le Bureau européen des unions de consommateurs (Beuc), qui ne participera pas à ce rapport, la prolifération des « fake news » est liée au modèle économique des plateformes, fondé sur les revenus publicitaires. Les chaînes privées ont, en revanche, salué l’initiative de la Commission.

Why Comcast Wants Sky
By Martin Peers  · 2 days ago ·  Source: The Information
Little wonder Brian Roberts is trying to buy Sky: He clearly needs to find something new for Comcast to do. The cable operator’s first-quarter results showed a company throwing off plenty of cash—$3.1 billion in free cash flow in the quarter—as capex dropped 5%. But those healthy results belie some worrying long-term trends. The number of video customers continues to decline while price increases on broadband helped drive revenue growth in what is becoming Comcast’s main business.

Comcast also got a boost from its television unit’s coverage of the Olympics. Of course, that only happens once every couple of years. As for broadband, there’s only so many times Comcast can ram through price rises before it gets heat from customers, which could rebound among politicians. That leaves Sky.

Comcast has formalized its takeover offer for Sky, one that is higher than 21st Century Fox’s regulator-stalled offer for all of Sky. MoffettNathanson analyst Craig Moffett has speculated that Sky would help Comcast start a global video streaming service to compete with Netflix. That explains why Sky may be more critical to Comcast than first appeared. Still, what matters now is what Disney and its to-be-acquired partner Fox does. Disney could bid for the 60% of Sky that Fox doesn’t own to ensure it gets control. This old-media deal is getting interesting.

BuzzFeed News Announces Netflix Show
By Tom Dotan  · 2 days ago ·  Source: Hollywood Reporter
Are journalists interesting? That’s the gamble Netflix is taking with its new series “Follow This” where it follows BuzzFeed journalists as they report on their stories. Coupled with Showtime’s upcoming series on the New York Times, we’ll soon be awash in scenes where people carry out visually stimulating tasks like typing, making calls and looking at computer monitors. Truly, this is a golden age.

In seriousness, this Netflix series could provide a new revenue outlet for BuzzFeed’s news operations. The large and ambitious division has until now been more of a cost center for the company. Management at BuzzFeed have been trying to figure out ways to generate revenue from it. Last year BuzzFeed began posting banner ads around their stories and started a morning ad-supported news show on Twitter. 

If this show works and BuzzFeed can spin up a few others, then licensing revenue could go a long way in subsidizing news and alleviating a major problem. The company has been adept at churning out short-form video that does well on Facebook and YouTube; it will be interesting if it can make that work within the Netflix on-demand model.

Researchers Find Alexa Security Flaw to Spy on Users
By Priya Anand  · 2 days ago ·  Source: Threatpost
Security researchers at the firm Checkmarx said they found a way to create an Alexa skill that would continue listening to users long after they prompted the software, according to Threatpost. The rogue skill could then send a recording and transcript of any audio to its creators.

Amazon said it has fixed the flaw, which researchers reported to the company before going public with their findings. It’s worth noting that Checkmarx didn’t try to push its skill through Amazon’s certification system to make it available to the public, so we can’t be sure as to whether Amazon has controls in place to avoid skills like this from slipping through. For those interested in learning exactly how the researchers were able to manipulate Alexa, it’s worth reading the whole story.

Regardless, it is troubling that Amazon did not previously have adequate safeguards in place to prevent a skill from continuing to listen to users. The public and lawmakers are questioning how social media companies (namely Facebook) collect and use consumer data. This kind of oversight makes it hard not to wonder when the scrutiny will reach Amazon, which is pushing the envelope to find new ways to collect data on its users, particularly inside their homes.

Twitter Posts Good Earnings, Stock Drops
By Tom Dotan  · 2 days ago ·  Source: The Information
For the first quarter of 2018, Twitter posted an increase in daily users, international revenue growth and its second straight profitable quarter. These are good earnings, prima facie, and should have sent the company’s stock up. Yet it fell 2.4% (although it recovered a little after hours.) Why? The business in the US is looking slow. Monthly active user growth was essentially flat and revenue was only up 2%.

Also executives implied in the shareholder letter that growth rates for the rest of the year would begin to slow. Twitter’s recovery began taking place midway through last year, which will now make the year-over-year comparisons less favorable.

Twitter stock has been on an upward swing over the last year, which perhaps has investors thinking its expensive at this point. This isn’t bad news—Twitter has emerged from the death spiral of the last few years to position itself as a sustainable business, if not a particularly large one. But as the earnings and investor response shows, it will need ongoing and bigger shift to fundamentally change people’s mindset.

Facebook Shifts U.S. Privacy Leadership
By Cory Weinberg and Sarah Kuranda  · 2 days ago ·  Source: The New York Times
Facebook is shuffling its top privacy leadership, with The New York Times reporting on Tuesday that it has replaced Vice President of U.S. Public Policy Erin Egan. Ms. Egan isn’t leaving the company entirely. A spokesman told the New York Times that she would stay on as chief privacy officer. People close to the company said Ms. Egan was well respected as a privacy expert (she has been widely recognized as a privacy and data security lawyer), but that she appeared to become less influential internally in recent months. It’s a crucial time for Facebook’s privacy team as the company is already facing steep criticisms around its handling of user data. 

The report didn’t explain the reason behind the shift, only that Ms. Egan would be replaced on an interim basis by Kevin Martin, reporting to Vice President of Global Public Policy Joel Kaplan.
